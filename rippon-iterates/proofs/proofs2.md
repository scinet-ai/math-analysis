# Rippon 7.54, round 2: the Koenigs band decomposition, the transient-line theorem, and the analytic profile

**Problem** (Hayman–Lingham 7.54, P. J. Rippon). $\varphi_t(z)=e^{tz}-1$,
$f_n(t):=\varphi_t^{n}(-1)$, $c^{(n)}_k:=[t^k]f_n$. Is $|c^{(n)}_k|\le1$ for all $n\ge1,k\ge0$?

**Round 1** (finding cda0ff02) proved: $f_n$ has valuation $n$; $c^{(n)}_n=-1$ (Lemma A);
diagonal stabilization $c^{(n)}_{n+j}=A_j$ for $n\ge\max(j,1)$ (Lemma B), where
$\Phi(t)=\sum_j A_j t^j$ is the *stable profile*; and the reduction of the conjecture to
(I) $|A_j|\le1\ \forall j$ and (II) $|c^{(n)}_k|\le1$ for $k>2n$.

**This round** upgrades the structure decisively. We prove that the *entire* coefficient
array is generated by a single hierarchy of universal power series ("band profiles"), one
for each band $m$, via a formal Koenigs linearization. Statement (II) thereby loses its
special status: every coefficient is a finite sum of band-profile coefficients. We also
prove an exact product factorization of the iterates, locate the analytic profile's
behavior on the real axis, certify $\Phi(-1/2)<-1$ rigorously (killing sup-norm routes at
the profile level), and delimit what remains open in statement (I).

Throughout, $f_0:=-1$ (the $0$-th iterate), $f_{n+1}=e^{tf_n}-1$, and
$$h(x):=\frac{e^x-1}{x}=\sum_{m\ge0}\frac{x^m}{(m+1)!}\in\mathbb{Q}[[x]],\qquad h(0)=1 .$$

## 1. Theorem 1 (exact factorization of the iterates)

> **Theorem 1.** For every $n\ge0$,
> $$f_n(t)\;=\;-\,t^{\,n}\prod_{k=0}^{n-1}h\bigl(t\,f_k(t)\bigr).$$
> Consequently the infinite product
> $$\Phi(t)\;=\;-\prod_{k=0}^{\infty}h\bigl(t\,f_k(t)\bigr)$$
> converges in the $t$-adic topology and equals the stable profile of Lemma B; in
> particular Lemma B (stabilization, with threshold $n\ge j$) follows anew.

*Proof.* Since $e^x-1=x\,h(x)$ as formal power series and $tf_n$ has positive valuation
($\mathrm{val}(tf_n)=n+1$), the recurrence reads
$f_{n+1}=e^{tf_n}-1=t\,f_n\,h(tf_n)$.
By induction from $f_0=-1$ (empty product):
$f_{n+1}=t\cdot\bigl(-t^{n}\prod_{k<n}h(tf_k)\bigr)h(tf_n)=-t^{\,n+1}\prod_{k<n+1}h(tf_k)$.

For the product formula: $h(tf_k)=1+O(t^{k+1})$ because $\mathrm{val}(tf_k)=k+1$ and
$h(x)-1\in x\mathbb{Q}[[x]]$. Hence the partial products form a $t$-adically Cauchy
sequence and, for any $m\le n$,
$$-\,t^{-n}f_n=\prod_{k<n}h(tf_k)\equiv\prod_{k<m}h(tf_k)\pmod{t^{m+1}} .$$
So $t^{-n}f_n \bmod t^{m+1}$ is independent of $n\ge m$: this is exactly stabilization
($c^{(n)}_{n+j}$ independent of $n$ for $n\ge j$), and the limit series is
$\Phi=-\prod_{k\ge0}h(tf_k)$. $\square$

*Verification.* The product (truncated at $k<J$) reproduces $A_0..A_{120}$ exactly
(`rippon/product_form.py`, 0 mismatches).

## 2. Formal substitution toolkit

We work in $R=\mathbb{Q}[[t]]$ with the $t$-adic (complete, ultrametric) topology. A
family $(x_i)_{i\in I}$ in $R$ is *summable* iff for every $V$ only finitely many $x_i$
have $\mathrm{val}\,(x_i)\le V$; sums of summable families exist and can be regrouped and
reordered arbitrarily (ultrametric Fubini). We use two standard consequences:

> **Lemma S (substitution).** Let $F(w)=\sum_m F_m w^m\in R[[w]]$ and $w_0\in R$ be such
> that $\mathrm{val}(F_m w_0^m)\to\infty$. Then $F(w_0):=\sum_m F_m w_0^m$ is a
> well-defined element of $R$; if $F=G$ in $R[[w]]$ then $F(w_0)=G(w_0)$ whenever both
> sides are defined (the summed families are identical).

> **Lemma C (associativity/composition).** Let $F(w)=\sum_m F_m w^m\in R[[w]]$,
> $g\in R[[z]]$ (or $g \in R$), and suppose the doubly-indexed family
> $\{F_m\cdot g_{i_1}\cdots g_{i_m}\, z^{i_1+\dots+i_m}\}$ (over $m$ and multi-indices) is
> summable. Then $(F\circ g)$ computed by expanding and collecting powers of $z$, and then
> substituting, equals $F(g(z_0))$ computed by first evaluating $g$: both equal the sum of
> the same summable family grouped two ways.

*Proof of both:* ultrametric Fubini for summable families in a complete ultrametric
abelian group. $\square$

The only nontrivial applications below have $\mathrm{val}\,F_m\ge m-1$ and
$\mathrm{val}(g_i\,\cdot)\ \ge i$ terms, for which the summability hypothesis is
immediate (the $(m,\vec\imath)$ term has valuation $\ge m-1+\sum i_j$).

## 3. Theorem 2 (formal Koenigs linearization at multiplier $t$)

> **Theorem 2.** There is a unique $P(w)=w+\sum_{m\ge2}p_m(t)\,w^m$ with
> $p_m\in\mathbb{Q}[[t]]$ satisfying, in $\mathbb{Q}[[t]][[w]]$,
> $$P(tw)\;=\;e^{t\,P(w)}-1 .$$
> Moreover $\mathrm{val}_t(p_m)\ge m-1$, and the first coefficients are
> $$p_2=\frac{t}{2(t-1)},\qquad p_3=\frac{t^2(t+2)}{6(t-1)^2(t+1)}$$
> (rational functions of $t$, regular at $t=0$; poles only at roots of unity).

*Proof.* Compare coefficients of $w^m$. With $[w^m]P^r=\sum_{m_1+\cdots+m_r=m,\ m_i\ge1}
\prod_i p_{m_i}$ ($p_1:=1$), the equation reads
$$t^m p_m=\sum_{r\ge1}\frac{t^r}{r!}\,[w^m]P^r
= t\,p_m+\sum_{r=2}^{m}\frac{t^r}{r!}[w^m]P^r ,$$
i.e.
$$(t^m-t)\,p_m=\sum_{r=2}^{m}\frac{t^r}{r!}\sum_{\substack{m_1+\cdots+m_r=m\\ m_i\ge1}}
p_{m_1}\cdots p_{m_r}=:F_m,\tag{$*$}$$
and $F_m$ involves only $p_2,\dots,p_{m-1}$ (all $m_i<m$ when $r\ge2$). Now
$t^m-t=-t\,(1-t^{m-1})$ with $1-t^{m-1}$ a unit of $\mathbb{Q}[[t]]$, and every term of
$F_m$ carries $t^r$, $r\ge2$, so $F_m\in t^2\mathbb{Q}[[t]]$; hence $p_m=F_m/(t^m-t)$ is a
well-defined element of $t\,\mathbb{Q}[[t]]$, uniquely determined by induction.

Valuation: assume $\mathrm{val}\,p_{m'}\ge m'-1$ for $m'<m$ (true for $p_1$). A term of
$F_m$ has valuation $\ge r+\sum_i(m_i-1)=r+(m-r)=m$; dividing by $t\cdot(\text{unit})$
gives $\mathrm{val}\,p_m\ge m-1$.

Explicit values from $(*)$: $m=2$: $(t^2-t)p_2=t^2/2$, so $p_2=t/(2(t-1))$.
$m=3$: $(t^3-t)p_3=t^2\,p_2+t^3/6=\frac{t^3}{2(t-1)}+\frac{t^3}{6}
=\frac{t^3(t+2)}{6(t-1)}$, so $p_3=\frac{t^2(t+2)}{6(t-1)^2(t+1)}$. $\square$

*Remark (small divisors).* $(*)$ divides by $t^{m}-t$; over $\mathbb{C}$ this vanishes at
$t^{m-1}=1$ — the formal calculation sidesteps the analytic small-divisor problem because
$1-t^{m-1}$ is a $t$-adic unit, but the poles of $p_m$ at roots of unity are real and
resurface in the analysis of §6.

## 4. Theorem 3 (band decomposition of the coefficient array)

Let $K(z)=z+\sum_{m\ge2}\kappa_m(t)z^m$ be the compositional inverse of $P$ in
$R[[z]]$; comparing $K(P(w))=w$ coefficientwise gives, by the same induction as in
Theorem 2, $\kappa_m\in\mathbb{Q}[[t]]$ with $\mathrm{val}\,\kappa_m\ge m-1$.

> **Theorem 3.** Define $\Psi_m:=p_m\,\Phi^m\in\mathbb{Q}[[t]]$ ($p_1:=1$, so
> $\Psi_1=\Phi$). Then, $t$-adically in $\mathbb{Q}[[t]]$, for every $n\ge1$:
> $$f_n(t)\;=\;\sum_{m\ge1}p_m(t)\,t^{mn}\,\Phi(t)^m ,$$
> where the $m$-th summand has valuation $\ge mn+m-1$. Consequently, for all $n,k$:
> $$c^{(n)}_k\;=\;\sum_{m=1}^{M(n,k)}\bigl[t^{\,k-mn}\bigr]\Psi_m,\qquad
> M(n,k)=\Bigl\lfloor\tfrac{k+1}{n+1}\Bigr\rfloor$$
> (the sum is empty beyond $m(n+1)-1\le k$, i.e. terms with $mn+m-1>k$ vanish).
> In particular **Rippon's conjecture is equivalent to**: for all $n\ge1$, $k\ge n$,
> $$\Bigl|\sum_{m\ge1,\ m(n+1)\le k+1}[t^{\,k-mn}]\Psi_m\Bigr|\le1 .$$

*Proof.* Set $\Phi^*:=K(-1)=\sum_m\kappa_m(t)(-1)^m$ (with $\kappa_1=1$), summable since
$\mathrm{val}\,\kappa_m\ge m-1\to\infty$; note $\Phi^*(0)=-1$ since
$\kappa_m(0)=0$ for $m\ge2$.

*Step 1: $f_n=P(t^n\Phi^*)$ for all $n\ge0$.*
Base $n=0$: $P(\Phi^*)=P(K(-1))=-1=f_0$. Indeed, apply Lemma C to the identity
$(P\circ K)(z)=z$ in $R[[z]]$ with the substitution $z=-1$. The relevant doubly-indexed
family is $\bigl\{p_m\,\kappa_{i_1}\cdots\kappa_{i_m}\,(-1)^{\,i_1+\dots+i_m}\bigr\}$
over $m\ge1$ and $\vec\imath\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge1}^m$ ($\kappa_1:=1$), with
$$\mathrm{val}\ \ge\ (m-1)+\textstyle\sum_j(i_j-1).$$
This family is summable: if the valuation is $\le V$ then $m\le V+1$ (finitely many
$m$), and for each such $m$ the excess $\sum_j(i_j-1)\le V$ means at most $V$ of the
$i_j$ exceed $1$ and each is $\le V+1$ — finitely many $\vec\imath$ per $m$. Grouping by
total degree $N=\sum i_j$ gives $\sum_N[z^N](P\circ K)\,(-1)^N=-1$ (the series is $z$);
grouping by $m$ first gives $P(K(-1))$. By ultrametric Fubini both equal the family's
sum, so $P(\Phi^*)=-1$.

Induction step: assume $f_n=P(t^n\Phi^*)$; here $w_n:=t^n\Phi^*$ has
$\mathrm{val}\,(w_n)=n$. For $n\ge0$, substitute $w=w_n$ in the defining identity
$P(tw)=e^{tP(w)}-1$ of Theorem 2 (Lemma S; for $n\ge1$ this is ordinary composition of
formal series of positive valuation; for $n=0$ the summability is as in the base case):
$$P(t\,w_n)=e^{t\,P(w_n)}-1=e^{t f_n}-1=f_{n+1},$$
and $t\,w_n=t^{n+1}\Phi^*=w_{n+1}$. So $f_{n+1}=P(w_{n+1})$.

*Step 2: expansion and $\Phi^*=\Phi$.* $f_n=P(t^n\Phi^*)=\sum_m p_m t^{mn}\Phi^{*m}$,
$t$-adically summable with $m$-th term of valuation $\ge mn+(m-1)$. Reducing mod
$t^{2n+1}$ leaves only $m=1$: $f_n\equiv t^n\Phi^*\pmod{t^{2n+1}}$, so
$c^{(n)}_{n+j}=[t^j]\Phi^*$ for $j\le n$; by Lemma B (or Theorem 1) the left side is
$A_j$ for $n\ge j$, hence $[t^j]\Phi^*=A_j$ for every $j$: $\Phi^*=\Phi$.

*Step 3: coefficient formula.* Extract $[t^k]$: the $m$-th band contributes
$[t^{k-mn}](p_m\Phi^m)$, which vanishes when $k-mn<\mathrm{val}\,\Psi_m$; since
$\mathrm{val}\,\Psi_m=\mathrm{val}\,p_m\ge m-1$, bands with $mn+m-1>k$ drop out. The
equivalence statement is a restatement using $\Psi_m$. $\square$

*Remark.* $\mathrm{val}\,p_2=1$, $\mathrm{val}\,p_3=2$ are exact, and the verified
threshold-tightness data (§5) shows $\mathrm{val}\,\Psi_3=2$ exactly; we only need the
inequality in general.

## 5. Theorem 4 (transient-line theorem — band 2, and the verified band 3)

Write $B_l:=[t^l]\Phi^2$ and
$$\tau_i:=[t^i]\Psi_2=[t^i]\Bigl(\tfrac{t}{2(t-1)}\Phi^2\Bigr)
=-\tfrac12\sum_{l=0}^{i-1}B_l ,\qquad
\sigma_i:=[t^i]\Psi_3 .$$

> **Theorem 4.** For all $n\ge1$ and $0\le i\le n+1$:
> $$c^{(n)}_{2n+i}\;=\;A_{n+i}+\tau_i .$$
> For all $n\ge1$ and $0\le i\le n+2$:
> $$c^{(n)}_{3n+i}\;=\;A_{2n+i}+\tau_{n+i}+\sigma_i .$$

*Proof.* Both are instances of Theorem 3. For $k=2n+i$ with $i\le n+1$: band $m\ge3$
requires $k\ge mn+m-1\ge3n+2>2n+i$, so only $m=1,2$ contribute, giving
$[t^{n+i}]\Phi+[t^i]\Psi_2$. For $k=3n+i$ with $i\le n+2$: band $m\ge4$ requires
$k\ge4n+3>3n+i$, giving $[t^{2n+i}]\Phi+[t^{n+i}]\Psi_2+[t^i]\Psi_3$. $\square$

**Corollaries (all new closed forms).**
1. $c^{(n)}_{2n+1}=A_{n+1}-\tfrac12$ for **all** $n\ge1$ ($\tau_1=-\tfrac12 B_0=-\tfrac12$).
2. $c^{(n)}_{2n+2}=A_{n+2}$ for all $n\ge1$ ($\tau_2=-\tfrac12(B_0+B_1)=-\tfrac12(1+2A_0A_1)=0$).
3. $c^{(n)}_{2n+3}=A_{n+3}+\tfrac{5}{24}$ for all $n\ge2$.
4. The round-1 extremal mystery is solved: the unique non-leading maximum
   $|c^{(6)}_{13}|=\tfrac{2663}{4480}$ sits on the line $k=2n+1$ and equals
   $\bigl|A_7-\tfrac12\bigr|=\tfrac12+\tfrac{423}{4480}$ ($A_7=-\tfrac{423}{4480}<0$);
   it is the point where $A_{n+1}$ is most negative. The "transient ridge"
   $c^{(n)}_{2n+1}\to-\tfrac12$ as $n\to\infty$ because $A_{n+1}\to0$.
5. On $k\le 3n+1$, Rippon's conjecture is **equivalent to**
   $|A_j|\le1$ (all $j$) **and** $|A_{n+i}+\tau_i|\le1$ (all $n\ge1,\ 0\le i\le n+1$);
   both hold throughout the verified window, and the second would follow for $n+i\ge2$
   from the two *profile bounds* $|A_j|\le\tfrac12\,(j\ge2)$ and $|\tau_i|\le\tfrac12$
   observed in all computed data.

**Exact verification** (`rippon/bands.py`, window $n\le60$, exact rationals):
band-2 formula on 1950 cells — 0 failures; band-3 formula on 2010 cells — 0 failures;
threshold tightness: at $i=n+2$ the band-2 formula fails by exactly $\sigma_2=-\tfrac13$
(the onset of band 3), for every tested $n$. Data: $\max_i|\tau_i|=\tfrac12$ (only at
$i=1$; second largest $\tfrac{5}{24}$), $\max_i|\sigma_i|=\tfrac13$ (only at $i=2$),
both computed to $i\le600$ with $\tau_i,\sigma_i\to0$.

## 6. Theorem 5 (the analytic profile) and the certified obstruction

Now regard $t$ as a complex number. Every $f_n$ is entire; $g_n(t):=t^{-n}f_n(t)$ is
entire (valuation $n$ at $0$).

> **Theorem 5.**
> (a) For every $\rho<\ln 2$, $g_n\to G$ uniformly on $\{|t|\le\rho\}$, where $G$ is
> holomorphic and equals the sum of the (convergent) series $\sum_j A_jt^j$. Hence the
> radius of convergence of $\Phi$ satisfies $R\ge\ln 2=0.6931\ldots$
> (b) For every $t\in(0,1)$: $f_k(t)\in(-1,0)$ for all $k\ge1$, $f_k(t)\uparrow 0$, the
> product $-\prod_{k\ge0}h(tf_k(t))$ converges, its value $G(t)$ lies in $(-1,0)$, and
> $g_n(t)\to G(t)$. Thus the (analytically continued) profile obeys the conjectured
> bound $|G|<1$ on the whole real segment $(0,1)$.
> (c) (Certified) $\Phi(-\tfrac12)<-1$ — note $|-\tfrac12|<\ln2\le R$, so this is a
> statement about the sum of the series itself. Consequently, by the maximum principle,
> $M(r):=\max_{|t|=r}|\Phi|\ \ge\ |\Phi(-\tfrac12)|>1.159$ for **every** $r\in[\tfrac12,R)$.
> Cauchy's estimate $|A_j|\le M(r)\,r^{-j}$ therefore cannot prove statement (I) even in
> the limit $r\uparrow R$: the trivial obstruction $M(r)\ge|\Phi(0)|=1$ might have been
> consistent with an Abel-type argument ($M(r)\to1$, $|A_j|\le\liminf M(r)r^{-j}=1$), but
> the certificate rules that out — $M(r)$ stays $\ge1.159$, bounded away from $1$.

*Proof.* (a) For $|t|\le\rho$, $|f_1|=|e^{-t}-1|\le e^{\rho}-1=:x_0$ and inductively
$|f_{k+1}|\le e^{\rho|f_k|}-1$, so $|f_k|\le x_k$ where $x_{k+1}=e^{\rho x_k}-1$. If
$\rho<\ln2$ then $x_0=e^\rho-1<1$, and on $(0,1)$ we have $e^{\rho x}-1<x$ iff
$\rho<\log(1+x)/x$; since $\log(1+x)/x$ is decreasing on $(0,1)$ with infimum
$\log 2$ at $x=1$, and $\rho<\ln 2$, indeed $e^{\rho x}-1<x$ on $(0,x_0]$. So $(x_k)$
is strictly decreasing, $x_k\downarrow \ell$ with $\ell=e^{\rho \ell}-1$ and
$\ell\in[0,x_0)$; the only such fixed point is $\ell=0$. Moreover the decrease is
eventually geometric ($x_{k+1}/x_k\to\rho<1$... more precisely
$x_{k+1}\le \frac{e^{\rho x_k}-1}{x_k}x_k$ and $\frac{e^{\rho x}-1}{x}\downarrow\rho$ as
$x\downarrow0$, so for any $\lambda\in(\rho,1)$, $x_{k+1}\le\lambda x_k$ for large $k$),
whence $\sum_k x_k<\infty$. By Theorem 1,
$g_n(t)=-\prod_{k<n}h(tf_k(t))$ and $|h(x)-1|\le e^{|x|}-1\le |x|e^{|x|}$, so
$\sum_k\sup_{|t|\le\rho}|h(tf_k)-1|\le\sum_k \rho\,x_k\,e^{\rho x_k}<\infty$: the product
converges uniformly on $|t|\le\rho$ to a holomorphic limit $G$ with $G=\lim g_n$.
Cauchy's integral formula gives $[t^j]g_n\to[t^j]G$; but $[t^j]g_n=c^{(n)}_{n+j}=A_j$
for $n\ge j$ (Lemma B), so $[t^j]G=A_j$: $G$'s Taylor series at $0$ is $\Phi$, and it
converges on $|t|<\ln2$. Hence $R\ge\ln2$.

(b) Fix $t\in(0,1)$. $f_1=e^{-t}-1\in(-1,0)$. If $x\in(-1,0)$ then $tx\in(-1,0)$ and
$e^{tx}-1\in(e^{-t}-1,0)\subset(-1,0)$; moreover $e^{u}-1>u$ for $u\ne0$ gives
$e^{tx}-1>tx\ge x$ (as $x<0$, $t\le1$), so $f_{k+1}>f_k$: the sequence increases in
$(-1,0)$, converges to the unique fixed point $0$ of $z\mapsto e^{tz}-1$ in $(-1,0]$.
For every $x<0$, $h(x)=\frac{e^x-1}{x}\in(0,1)$: numerator and denominator are both
negative (so $h>0$), and $h(x)<1\iff e^x-1>x$ (dividing by $x<0$ flips the
inequality), which is the strict convexity bound $e^x>1+x$ ($x\ne0$). Hence every
partial product $\prod_{k<n}h(tf_k)\in(0,1)$ and $g_n(t)=-\prod_{k<n}h(tf_k)\in(-1,0)$
for $n\ge1$. Convergence of the full product: $f_{k+1}/f_k=t\,h(tf_k)\to t<1$, so
$|f_k|\to0$ geometrically and $\sum_k|tf_k|<\infty$; since each factor lies in $(0,1)$
and $\sum_k(1-h(tf_k))\le\sum_k|tf_k|<\infty$ (using $|1-h(x)|\le|x|$ for $|x|\le1$,
i.e. $e^u\le1+u+u^2$ on $[0,1]$), the infinite product converges to a positive limit; since every factor is $<1$, the
limit is $\le h(-t)<1$. So
$G(t):=\lim g_n(t)=-\prod_{k\ge0}h(tf_k(t))\in(-1,0)$.

(c) Computation `rippon/phi_cert.py` (python-flint `arb`, 256-bit balls, directed
rounding): the orbit $f_k(-1/2)$ is iterated as balls via $\texttt{expm1}$;
$g_{200}=f_{200}/t^{200}$ is a certified enclosure, and the analytic tail bound (proved
below) bounds $|G(-\tfrac12)-g_{200}|$. Output:
$$G(-\tfrac12)\in[-1.15904405095508\ldots,\;-1.15904405095507\ldots]\subset(-\infty,-1).$$
*Tail bound used:* once a ball certifies $|f_{k_0}|\le\tfrac18$ (here $k_0=4$), then for
$m\ge k_0$, $|f_{m+1}|\le e^{|tf_m|}-1\le|t||f_m|e^{|tf_m|}\le\tfrac12 e^{1/16}|f_m|
\le0.54\,|f_m|$, so $|x_m|:=|tf_m|\le\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac18\cdot0.54^{\,m-k_0}$ and
$S_N:=\sum_{m\ge N}|x_m|\le\tfrac1{16}\,0.54^{\,N-k_0}/0.46$. From
$g_M=g_N\prod_{m=N}^{M-1}h(x_m)$ and $|h(x)-1|\le e^{|x|}-1-|x|\le|x|$ (for $|x|\le1$):
$|G/g_N-1|\le e^{S_N}-1$, so $|G-g_N|\le|g_N|\,(e^{1.04\,S_N}-1)$ (the $1.04$ is slack).
With $N=200$ the tail is $<6\cdot10^{-54}$. $\square$

**Interpretation.** (b) and (c) together sharpen the round-1 "Route B fails" analysis to
the profile level and make it *rigorous*: the conjectured bound $|A_j|\le1$ cannot be an
$L^\infty$ phenomenon on circles — $|\Phi|$ exceeds $1$ at explicit interior points
($t=-\tfrac12$) — it must come from cancellation. Numerically the obstruction extends to
$L^1$: $\frac1{2\pi}\int|\Phi(re^{i\theta})|\,d\theta\approx1.02$–$1.13$ for
$r\in[0.5,0.985]$ (so the Hardy-space $H^1$ route fails as well, though only by
$\sim13\%$ — closer than any other classical route; see `boundary_probe.py`).

## 7. The boundary picture (numerics; conjectures for round 3)

All from 600 exact profile coefficients (`data/profile_J600.json`):

- **Radius of convergence.** $|A_j|^{1/j}$ climbs to $\approx0.990$ at $j=600$;
  envelope $\max|A_j|$ on blocks decays like $j^{-0.88}$. **Conjecture: $R=1$.**
- **Not holonomic.** No P-recurrence $\sum_{i\le d}P_i(j)A_{j+i}=0$ with $d\le8$,
  $\deg P_i\le 12-d$ fits 601 terms (`guess_holonomic.py`, exact arithmetic mod
  $2^{61}-1$ with validation rows). $\Phi$ is (empirically) **not P-recursive**,
  consistent with a natural boundary at $|t|=1$.
- **Parabolic boundary zeros.** Radial limits at root-of-unity directions vanish:
  $\Phi(r)\!\to\!0$, $\Phi(-r)\!\to\!0$, and $|\Phi(re^{2\pi i/q})|$ decreasing in $r$
  near $1$ for $q=3,4,5,6$. Explanation: at $t=e^{2\pi ip/q}$ the fixed point $0$ of
  $\varphi_t$ is parabolic; the orbit of $-1$ still converges to $0$ (inside a petal) but
  sub-geometrically, so $g_n=f_n/t^n\to0$ ($|t^n|=1$, $f_n\to0$). **Conjecture: $\Phi$
  has radial limit $0$ at every root of unity — a dense set of boundary zeros — and
  $|t|=1$ is a natural boundary.** This *explains* non-holonomicity (holonomic functions
  have finitely many singularities) and the failure of all single-contour methods.
- **Simple zero at $t=1$.** $\Phi(r)/(1-r)\to\approx-2.0$ as $r\to1^-$
  ($-1.96$ at $r=0.99$): $\Phi(t)\sim2(t-1)$, matching the classical parabolic orbit
  asymptotics $f_k(1)\sim-2/k$. Moreover all computed band profiles vanish at $1$:
  $\sum_l B_l\to\Phi(1)^2=0$ (partial sums $\to0$), $\tau_i\to0$, $\sigma_i\to0$ — the
  poles of $p_m$ at $t=1$ are cancelled by the zeros of $\Phi^m$. **Conjecture:
  $\Psi_m(t)\to0$ as $t\to1^-$ for every $m$** (verified numerically for $m\le3$).
- **Partial-sum law.** $S_J:=\sum_{j\le J}A_j$ satisfies $|S_J|\le1$ with equality only
  at $J=0$, $|S_J|<0.007$ for $J\ge50$ — the coefficients of $\Phi(t)/(1-t)$ obey the
  same "$\le1$, equality only at leading" law as everything else in this problem.
